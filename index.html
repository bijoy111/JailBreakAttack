Abstract

As Large Language Models (LLMs) become increasingly integrated into diverse applications, their susceptibility to misuse for generating harmful content poses significant security risks. This paper introduces novel jailbreak attacks designed to exploit these vulnerabilities by embedding harmful prompts within seemingly benign sequences, such as game environments, dialogue completions, and question banks. Our methodology leverages automated template-based formatting to obfuscate malicious content, requiring minimal manual effort and only black-box access. We evaluate the effectiveness of these attacks on both open-source and closed-source models, demonstrating their ability to bypass existing safety mechanisms. Our findings reveal critical gaps in current LLM defenses, underscoring the need for more robust security measures. This research contributes to a deeper understanding of LLM vulnerabilities and provides a foundation for developing more resilient safeguards.

Ethics and disclosure

                

                    This paper introduces the Sequential Break methodology for generating novel jailbreak prompts that exploit sequential structures in Larg Language Models(LLMs). While these techniques could potentially be leveraged by adversaries to bypass safety mechanisms, the primary focus of this research is on enhancing LLM security and resilience. By identifying these vulnerabilities, we aim to raise awareness with in the AI community and contribute to the development of more robust defenses strategies. Our intention is to advance the security of LLMs in real-world applications by uncovering critical weaknesses and suggesting improvements. We believe that by sharing these insights, we can help accelerate the redevelopment of stronger safeguards that protect LLMs from similar attack vectors. This research is ethically driven, prioritizing the safe and responsible use of LLMs across diverse applications and user communities. To ensure responsible dissemination our findings, we will collaborate with the broader AI and security communities, encouraging ongoing research into LLM safety and fostering a collective effort to build more secure and reliable AI systems.
